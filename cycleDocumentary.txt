The menstrual cycle is a natural biological process that occurs in the female reproductive system. It usually lasts about 28 days, but the length can vary from person to person. The menstrual cycle is divided into four main phases: 
1.The follicular phase 
2.ovulation
3.The luteal phase
4.Menstruation

The follicular phase. 
This phase usually lasts about two weeks and begins on the first day of the menstrual cycle. During this phase, the body prepares to release an egg from the ovaries. The ovaries produce a hormone called follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH), which stimulates the development of a follicle (a small fluid-filled sac) in the ovaries. The follicle contains an immature egg. As the follicle grows, it produces the hormone estrogen. The increasing levels of estrogen lead to the thickening of the lining of the uterus.

Ovulation. 
This phase lasts for about one to three days and occurs when the follicle in the ovaries releases a mature egg. After the egg is released, it travels through the fallopian tubes and into the uterus. The egg can be fertilized by sperm during this time. If the egg is not fertilized, it will dissolve and be reabsorbed by the body. Ovulation is usually when women are most fertile, meaning they have the highest chance of getting pregnant.

The luteal phase. 
This phase lasts about two weeks and starts after ovulation. During this phase, the body produces another hormone called progesterone, which helps to maintain the lining of the uterus. The progesterone also causes the body temperature to rise slightly. If the egg is not fertilized, the levels of estrogen and progesterone drop and the lining of the uterus breaks down and is shed through the vagina. This is what is known as menstruation, or a period.

The length of the menstrual cycle can vary from person to person and even from month to month. Some people have cycles that are shorter or longer than 28 days, and some have irregular cycles that don't follow a regular pattern.
